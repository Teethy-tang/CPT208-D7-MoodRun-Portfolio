1. Motivation & Research

Structured by coursework requirements: The Why, The Gap, The Stakeholders.

The Why (200 words)

We selected the Active Lifestyles track because many student runners start with good intentions but struggle to maintain consistency when stress, fatigue, or low confidence accumulates. Existing running apps mainly reward speed, distance, and competition, but daily motivation is often shaped by emotional readiness rather than physical ability alone. Our project, MoodRun, focuses on that missing layer: helping users decide how to run today based on current mood and context.

In our observations around campus running routes and informal peer conversations, we noticed that users frequently skipped planned sessions after intense coursework periods, even when they had enough time. They did not need stronger competition; they needed lower-friction support that felt human, encouraging, and adaptable. Therefore, our core design objective is to create a playful companion that adjusts guidance tone, challenge level, and reflection prompts to user emotions.

This direction is meaningful for human-centric computing because it treats wellbeing as part of performance instead of separate from it. By combining lightweight mood check-ins with adaptive recommendations, MoodRun aims to improve adherence, reduce discouragement, and support more inclusive running habits for users with different confidence levels and goals.

The Gap

4 academic papers + 4 commercial products; 3 strengths and 3 misses.

Academic Papers (A1-A4)

  • A1: Emotion-aware coaching in digital fitness (year, venue).
  • A2: Motivation and adherence in running apps (year, venue).
  • A3: Adaptive feedback timing for sports UX (year, venue).
  • A4: Wellbeing-centered persuasive design (year, venue).

3 Things They Did Well

  • Established evidence that emotional state affects exercise persistence.
  • Proposed useful adaptation strategies for feedback and encouragement.
  • Provided evaluation methods for short-term usability and engagement.

3 Things They Missed

  • Limited discussion of real-world, weekly usage continuity.
  • Insufficient integration with playful interaction design.
  • Few studies balance wellbeing and performance goals together.

Commercial Products (C1-C4)

  • C1: Nike Run Club.
  • C2: Strava.
  • C3: Adidas Running.
  • C4: Zombies, Run!

3 Things They Did Well

  • Strong activity tracking and progress visualization.
  • Large communities and social accountability mechanisms.
  • Some playful or narrative elements increase short-term interest.

3 Things They Missed

  • Guidance is mostly performance-first, not mood-first.
  • Limited emotional personalization before and during runs.
  • Reflection features are often shallow or optional add-ons.

The Stakeholders

Primary and secondary users with distinct personas.

Primary User Persona

Name: Lin, 20, undergraduate student runner.

Goal: Maintain 3 runs per week without burnout.

Pain Points: Stress after classes, guilt from missed runs, unstable motivation.

Needs: Quick mood-aware plans and non-judgmental in-run support.

Secondary User Persona

Name: Hao, 24, beginner fitness user.

Goal: Build a sustainable routine and confidence in public running spaces.

Pain Points: Anxiety about pace, comparison pressure, uncertainty about training load.

Needs: Playful encouragement, flexible targets, and positive reflection prompts.
